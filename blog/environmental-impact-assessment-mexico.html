Environmental Impact Assessment for Construction in Quintana Roo

Updated June 2026 • By Recrea Construction • 8 min read

Key fact: Most construction in Quintana Roo requires some form of environmental assessment. Building without one can result in fines, construction halt, and demolition orders.

What Is an MIA?

An MIA (Manifestacion de Impacto Ambiental) is Mexico's environmental impact assessment. It's a technical document that evaluates how a proposed construction project will affect the local environment and proposes mitigation measures. The MIA is submitted to SEMARNAT (Mexico's federal environmental agency) or to the state/municipal environmental authority, depending on the project's scope.

When Is an MIA Required?

In Quintana Roo, an MIA is typically required for:

  • Any construction in the coastal zone — within 20 meters of the beach or in the federal maritime-terrestrial zone (ZOFEMAT)
  • Projects in or near protected areas — national parks, biosphere reserves, natural protected areas (Sian Ka'an, Puerto Morelos Reef)
  • Projects near cenotes — construction within 50-100 meters of a cenote may require a federal MIA
  • Large-scale developments — residential developments over a certain number of units, commercial buildings over a certain size
  • Land clearing (desmonte) — removing native vegetation from undeveloped lots
  • Projects that affect mangroves — mangroves are federally protected in Mexico. Any impact on mangrove areas requires federal authorization.

The MIA Process

  1. Hire an environmental consultant — the MIA must be prepared by a certified environmental professional (perito ambiental). They conduct site surveys, ecological studies, and prepare the technical document.
  2. Baseline studies — the consultant documents existing vegetation, wildlife, soil conditions, hydrology, and any protected species on or near the site. In Quintana Roo, this often includes surveys for endangered species like the Yucatan black howler monkey, white-nosed coati, or specific bird species.
  3. Impact assessment — the document identifies potential environmental impacts of the construction (tree removal, soil disruption, water table effects, noise, light pollution) and proposes specific mitigation measures.
  4. Submission — the completed MIA is submitted to the appropriate authority (municipal, state, or federal).
  5. Review period — the authority reviews the MIA, may request additional information, and may conduct site inspections.
  6. Resolution — the authority issues an authorization (with conditions) or a denial. Conditions typically include specific mitigation measures, replanting requirements, and monitoring obligations.

Timeline

  • MIA preparation: 4–8 weeks (includes field work and document preparation)
  • Municipal ETJ review: 2–4 weeks
  • State MIA review: 2–3 months
  • Federal MIA review: 60 business days (approximately 3 months), but can extend if additional information is requested

Consequences of Building Without an MIA

  • Construction halt (clausura) — PROFEPA (environmental enforcement agency) can seal your construction site, halting all work indefinitely
  • Fines — environmental violations can result in fines of $50,000–$5,000,000 MXN depending on severity
  • Demolition order — in serious cases, PROFEPA can order the demolition of unauthorized construction at the owner's expense
  • Criminal charges — destruction of protected ecosystems (mangroves, protected species habitat) can result in criminal prosecution
  • Property title issues — construction without proper environmental authorization can affect your ability to sell or finance the property
